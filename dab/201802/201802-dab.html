Carlos Rinaldi Named Chair of the Department of Chemical Engineering

Dr. Rinaldi is the Charles A. Stokes Term Professor at the J. Crayton Pruitt Family Department of Biomedical Engineering. He is an internationally renowned scholar for his significant contributions to the field of bionanomedicine through the advancement of biomedical applications of magnetic nanoparticles, specifically cancer treatment through magnetic fluid hyperthermia. His scholarly achievements have earned him numerous awards, including the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers (PECASE), the International Journal of Nanomedicine Early Career Award for outstanding contributions to the field of nanomedicine, the Merck Health Innovation Award in Engineering and the NSF CAREER Award. At UF, Rinaldi is also recognized for his dedication and service to faculty and students â€“ he has been awarded a University of Florida Term Professorship, the Department of Chemical Engineering Excellence in Service Award, and the J. Crayton Pruitt Family Department of Biomedical Engineering Faculty Service Award. Carlos received his bachelor's degree in Chemical Engineering at the University of Puerto Rico, Mayagüez and completed degrees in Master of Science in Chemical Engineering, Master of Science in Chemical Engineering Practice and Doctor of Philosophy in Chemical Engineering at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. He was the Interim Chair at CHE since August 2017 and the Senior Associate Chair at BME from January 2016 to July 2017. Koch School of Chemical Engineering Practice and Professor at the Department of Chemical Engineering at the University of Puerto Rico, Mayagüez.

David Kaber From NCSU Appointed Chair of the Department of Industrial and Systems Engineering

The college is proud to announce the appointment of David Kaber, Ph.D., as Professor and Chair of the Department of Industrial and Systems Engineering. Dr. Kaber is currently a Distinguished Professor at North Carolina State University (NCSU) in the Edward P. Fitts Department of Industrial and Systems Engineering. He has been a member of the NCSU faculty for 17 years. He is currently the Director of Research at the Ergonomics Center of North Carolina (ECNC) and the Director of the Occupational Safety and Ergonomics (OSE) Program, both at NCSU. Under his leadership, the Ergonomics Center has been successful in gaining federal as well as industry-sponsored funding, including from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), Duke Energy, Strong Arm and John Deere. The OSE Program is an Education and Research Center (ERC) under the National Institute of Occupational Safety & Health (NIOSH) Southeastern region, where it has provided extensive student recruitment and training in the areas of cognitive ergonomics, physical ergonomics and occupational safety engineering for the past ten years. Dr. Kaber’s research interest is in human-systems engineering, including measures, models and methods (the 3 M’s) for human performance in complex systems. His current work focuses on modeling and analysis of cognitive workload and situation awareness in unmanned systems operations, driver performance and behavior in automated vehicle use, and design principles for “automation transparency” in human-in-the-loop systems. Dr. Kaber has published over 100 journal papers and 145 conference proceedings papers. He has supervised 20 doctoral students with at least six of these students currently working as university faculty members. His expertise has earned him multiple recognitions by his peers. These recognitions include being inducted as a Fellow into the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society and the Institute of Industrial Engineering. He has also earned numerous research grants from the NSF, NASA and the Federal Highway Administration. Dr. Kaber received his B.S. (1991) and M.S. (1993) degrees in industrial engineering from the University of Central Florida and a Ph.D. in industrial engineering from Texas Tech University in 1996. He is a certified safety professional (CSP) and certified human factors professional (CHFP). He will begin his tenure as the Chair of ISE on July 1.

Toshi Nishida Appointed Associate Dean for Academic Affairs

Dr. Nishida is a Professor in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering (ECE), and an Affiliate Professor in the Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering (MAE) at the University of Florida. He has published over 160 refereed journal and conference papers and received three best paper awards. He has mentored 23 Ph.D. and 23 M.S. students. His research focuses on high performance multifunctional semiconductor devices and systems, low power ferroelectric memory and device technologies, microelectromechanical systems, device reliability, and novel three-dimensional and flexible fabrication processes. He has 12 issued U.S. patents. Dr. Nishida is the Director of the NSF Industry/University Cooperative Research Center on Multi-functional Integrated System Technology (MIST) launched in 2014. He is a co-founder of the Interdisciplinary Microsystems Group, a 10-faculty, 80-member multidisciplinary group. He received the 2003 College of Engineering Teacher of the Year award, the 2017 University of Florida Term Professorship award, and is an Alan Hastings Faculty Fellow. He is a senior member of the IEEE. He received his Ph.D. (1988) and M.S. degrees in Electrical and Computer engineering and his B.S. degree in Engineering physics at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.

Christine Schmidt Reappointed to Another 5 Year Term as BME Chair

Dr. Schmidt continues her leadership as Chair of the J. Crayton Pruitt Family Department of Biomedical Engineering for the next five years. She joined UF in January 2013 from the University of Texas at Austin where she was a Professor in the Biomedical Engineering Department. Under her leadership, UF BME has grown significantly in stature and size, and is now ranked among the top 20 public BME departments in the country. Dr. Schmidt is an accomplished scholar. Her research focuses on engineering novel materials and therapeutic systems to stimulate damaged peripheral and spinal neurons to regenerate. Taking a unique approach to this problem, she uses electrically conducting polymers and natural-based materials to create therapies that can electrically, chemically, biologically and mechanically trigger neurons at both the macroscopic and nanometer-scales. She is the President-elect of the American Institute of Medical and Biological Engineering (AIMBE), a Fellow of AAAS, BMES, and AIMBE, serves on several editorial boards and has received the Distinguished Alumnus Award from the Cockrell School of Engineering at the University of Texas. Dr. Schmidt received her B.S. in Chemical Engineering from the University of Texas at Austin in 1988 and her Ph.D. in Chemical Engineering from the University of Illinois in 1995. She was an NIH Postdoctoral Fellow at MIT from 1994 to 1996.

FACULTY RESEARCH GRANTS

Mingzhou Ding, BME, in collaboration with the Department of Psychology’s Andreas Keil, won a NIH R01 project entitled “Emotional Engagement Driven by Complex Visual Stimuli: Neural Dynamics Revealed by Multimodal Imaging.” The $1.8M project will utilize multiple neuroimaging modalities and advanced computational methods to understand brain dynamics underlying emotional responses to pleasant, neutral, or unpleasant scenes. The ultimate goal of the research is to develop markers of emotional brain dysfunction that can be used in diagnosis and treatment.
