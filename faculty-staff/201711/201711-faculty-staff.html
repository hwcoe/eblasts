COLLEGE NEWS

UF announces largest fundraising campaign in university history

The University of Florida has undertaken the most ambitious fundraising campaign in its history to continue its rise as one of the nation’s leading public universities. The $3 billion initiative — tagged “Go Greater” — is among the largest active campaigns for a public university and almost double the $1.72 billion raised during UF’s previous campaign, which concluded in 2012.

College chairman Dr. Herbert Wertheim serves on the campaign’s leadership committee and spoke at the kickoff celebration on October 13. The $50 million gift to the college from Dr. Wertheim and his wife, Nicole, helped set the stage for UF to Go Greater.

FACULTY RESEARCH GRANTS

Norman Fitz-Coy (MAE) was funded by NASA and the Air Force for a 5-year, $3M project to characterize the debris fragments from the DebriSat and DebrisLV laboratory hypervelocity impact (HVI) experiments. The research will generate critical data required to improve existing spacecraft breakup models used in the assessment and prediction of the orbital debris environment. The ability to accurately predict the orbital debris population in low earth orbit is one of the critical components in space traffic management.

John Conklin (MAE) and Co-PIs Peter Wass (MAE) and Guido Mueller (Physics) won a $2.8M contract with NASA to develop the charge management system (CMS), a payload instrument for the Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA) comprised of three spacecraft that orbit the sun in formation. LISA will launch in the early 2030s and use precision inertial sensors and laser interferometry between each satellite to detect gravitational waves, e.g. those from merging supermassive black holes.

Benjamin Keselowsky and Gregory Hudalla, BME, were awarded a $2.1 million NIH grant to develop a new approach of administering enzymes to locally shut down the inflammation associated with periodontal infection.

Jennifer Andrew, ECE, David Arnold, ECE, and Carlos Rinaldi, CHE/BME, won a NSF Scalable Nanomanufacturing project for the Large-area Manufacturing of Integrated Devices with Nanocomposite Magnetic Cores. The $1.4M project will study synthetic and nanomanufacturing processes for the development of high-performance magnetic materials. This new approach may lead to the next generation of miniaturized power electronics that could be implemented into handheld and other portable electronic devices.

The NSF Major Research Instrumentation program selected FICS Research (Mark Tehranipoor, PI; Swarup Bhunia, Damon Woodard, Domenic Forte, Navid Asadi) to receive $1.3M for a photon emission and electro-optical microscope.

Yier Jin, ECE, was recently awarded with two NSF grants totaling $1.13M to study security certification of autonomous cyber-physical systems and reverse engineering.

Baba Vemuri, CISE, received a $1.06M grant from NSF’s Collaborative Research for Computational Neuroscience (CRCNS) program titled, “Automated Analysis of Movement Disorders from Diffusion and Functional MRI.” Vemuri, along with an interdisciplinary team of researchers from Statistics (Dr. Hani Doss), Neurology (Dr. Michael Okun) and Kinesiology (Dr. David Vaillancourt) will be developing novel algorithms for detecting subtle effects that can be revealed by Magnetic Resonance Brain scans, including changes in water diffusional properties of human brain tissue, and functional brain activity. Assessing such changes will facilitate development of automated methods to discriminate between different types of movement disorders such as the Parkinson's disease and essential tremor.
