UF Faculty Receive PECASE Award

Three engineering faculty received the prestigious PECASE awards: Domenic Forte, ECE, Aysegul Gunduz, BME, and Maitane Olabarrieta, ESSIE. The PECASE, or the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers, is the highest honor bestowed by the United States government on outstanding early-career scientists and engineers.

College Launches Department of Engineering Education

The Herbert Wertheim College of Engineering Department of Engineering Education is the college’s tenth department and will be led by Hans van Oostrom. The department specializes in engineering education research and the delivery of innovative instructional methods in engineering undergraduate courses as well as assessment.

David Hahn, MAE department chair, has accepted the position of Dean of Engineering at the University of Arizona. Under Dr. Hahn’s leadership, the department has made many great advances including the addition of a 4,000 sq. ft. space dedicated to student design-and-build activities.

FACULTY RESEARCH GRANTS

Tommy Angelini Awarded $4.4 Million for Advanced Liver 3D Tissue Models

Tommy Angelini, MAE, led a team of seven other investigators to win a $4.4M, two-year award from the Advanced Regenerative Manufacturing Institute (ARMI). Angelini and his team will create small-scale models of liver tissue using a 3D bioprinting technique developed in his lab.

UF To Lead AFOSR Center Of Excellence For Assured Autonomy

With up to $6 million in support from the Air Force Office of Scientific Research (AFOSR), the Center of Excellence for Assured Autonomy in Contested Environments at UF will be led by Warren Dixon, MAE, and the Florida Institute for National Security and includes collaboration with MAE, ECE, CISE, and the Air Force Research Laboratories and other universities.

Domenic Forte Receives $1M ECASE-ARMY Award

Domenic Forte, ECE, received the Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers (PECASE-ARMY). Forte’s $1 million grant will help his team safeguard hardware systems by developing a unique “fingerprint” to prevent malicious vandalism in the supply chain process.

NIH Awards $2.4M to Interdisciplinary Team To Improve Perception and Control of Prosthetic Limbs

Jack Judy, ECE, and Co-PIs Carlos Rinaldi, CHE, Christine Schmidt, BME, and Kevin Otto, BME, won a $2.4M grant from the NIH to develop tissue-engineered electronic nerve interfaces (TEENI) that are designed to help improve the ability of amputees to sense and control even the most advanced state-of-the-art prosthetic limbs with integrated sensory feedback.

Kyla McMullen Receives NSF Early Career Award To Enhance First Responder Effectiveness

With a system developed by Kyla McMullen, CISE, responders would hear unique 3D audio cues representing the locations of targets of interest, such as victims, exits, fire panels and water sources.

McMullen also received a $922K grant from the NSF which will provide support to Ph.D. students facing socioeconomic barriers to degree completion and career outlook.


Kyla McMullen

UF Engineering Interdisciplinary Team Awarded $2 Million NSF Grant for Smart Traffic

Sanjay Ranka, CISE, leads the $2M NSF award with colleagues Lily Elefteriadou, ESSIE, Anand Rangarajan, CISE, and Sivaramakrishnan Srinivasan, CISE. The project aims to further the ability of cities and communities to deploy technology that saves lives by studying “near miss” incidents to design safer transportation systems.

Blanka Sharma Receives NSF Early Career Award for Cancer Research

Blanka Sharma, BME, will study natural killer cells and their ability to penetrate the physical and biochemical barriers in tumors in order to destroy them.

Roozbeh Tabrizian receives DARPA Young Faculty Award

Roozbeh Tabrizian, ECE, has received a 2019 DARPA Young Faculty Award for the project entitled “Ferroelectrically Transduced Ge Nano-Fin Bulk Acoustic Resonators for Chip-Scale Instinctually Adaptive RF Spectral Processing.” The $500k award will enable the first nano-scale hardware to instinctually detect and suppress arbitrary jamming signals prior to entering radio frequency wireless receivers. Benefiting from the radically miniaturized size, weight, and power consumption, this technology will enhance ultra-compact tactical systems, such as nano-scale unmanned aerial vehicles, with jamming-immune access to satellites for wideband communication.
